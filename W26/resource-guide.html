How to Find Out What People Write in a Field

ENGL 1181 — Resource Guide

Why this matters

Every profession has its own genres — types of writing with specific conventions, purposes, and audiences. A paralegal writes very different things than a nurse or a marketing manager, and those differences tell us something about what each field values. For your genre analysis essay, you'll be analyzing one specific genre from your career field. But first, you need to find out what's out there.

You're going to be exploring the kinds of writing that professionals produce in a specific career. This guide walks you through several strategies for figuring out what those documents are and finding examples of them.

Strategy 1: Search Directly

Try searching for phrases like:

  • "What do [job title]s write?" or "[job title] writing responsibilities"
  • "Types of documents [job title]s prepare"
  • "[job title] daily tasks writing"

For example: "what do paralegals write" or "paralegal writing responsibilities" will likely return articles, blog posts, or professional association pages that describe common writing tasks.

Strategy 2: Look at Job Postings

Search for job postings for your target career on sites like Indeed, LinkedIn, or Glassdoor. Read the "Responsibilities" and "Qualifications" sections — they often list specific types of documents you'd be expected to produce.

How to do this

Go to a job search site and type in the job title you're researching. You don't need to be job hunting — you're reading these like research documents. Open several postings (aim for at least 5-6) and look for language about writing, preparing, drafting, or reviewing documents. The exact document names that show up — things like "draft correspondence," "prepare discovery requests," or "maintain case files" — are what you're looking for.

Pay attention to patterns. If you see the same type of document mentioned across multiple postings, that's a strong signal that it's a core part of the job. You may also notice that different levels of the same job (entry-level vs. senior) involve different types of writing, which is worth noting.

Tips

  • Don't just search the exact job title. Try related titles too. For example, if you're researching paralegal writing, you might also look at postings for "legal assistant" or "litigation support specialist," since these roles overlap.
  • If a posting mentions a document type you've never heard of, write it down and search for it separately. That's a lead.

Strategy 3: Check Professional Program Websites

Look at the websites for programs that train people in your field. Their course descriptions often list the types of writing students learn to produce, which tells you what the profession considers essential.

How to do this

Search for something like "[job title] certificate program" or "[field] associate degree courses." You're looking for the program's course catalog or list of required classes. Once you find a program page, look for individual course descriptions — these usually include a list of skills or assignments, and many of them will name specific document types. For example, a paralegal program might list a course called "Legal Research and Writing" whose description mentions memoranda, briefs, and client letters.

Check a few different programs (at least 2-3), since they won't all emphasize the same things. Community colleges, universities, and professional certification organizations are all good places to look.

Tips

  • If a program website has a "What You'll Learn" or "Program Outcomes" section, start there — it often gives you a quick overview of the key writing tasks.
  • Professional associations sometimes publish their own curriculum guidelines or competency standards, which can be even more specific. Try searching for "[field] professional association" or "[field] competency standards."

Strategy 4: Find Sample Documents

Search for examples of specific document types. Many organizations post templates, guides, or sample documents. Searching for "[document type] example" or "[document type] template" can turn up real examples.

How to do this

By the time you get to this step, you should have a list of document names from Strategies 1-3. Now you're going to search for actual examples of those documents so you can see what they look like. Take one document type at a time and try searches like:

  • "[document type] example"
  • "[document type] template"
  • "[document type] sample PDF"
  • "how to write a [document type]"

For example, if your earlier research told you that paralegals write demand letters, you might search for "demand letter example" or "demand letter template." You'll often find law firm websites, legal aid organizations, or writing guides that include full or partial samples.

Not every search will turn up a clean, complete example — and that's okay. Templates are useful too, because they show you the expected structure and sections even if the content is placeholder text. Writing guides are also valuable because they often walk through what each section of the document is supposed to do.

Tips

  • Government agencies and nonprofit organizations are especially good sources for sample documents, since they often publish templates for public use.
  • If you search for a document type and get no results, try rephrasing. Professional fields sometimes use different names for the same document. For example, a "legal memorandum" might also be called a "memo of law" or an "internal memo."
  • When you find an example, save or bookmark it. You'll want to be able to refer back to it later.

What to Do with What You Find

Take notes as you explore. Jot down the names of document types, who writes them, who reads them, and anything you notice about how they look or what they seem to be for. You'll use these notes for Reflection 2 and to help you choose a genre for your essay.
