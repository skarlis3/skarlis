Methods for Getting Started

There's no single right way to start a draft. Here are two approaches — use whichever one feels more natural, or combine them.

Freewriting

Set a timer (15–20 minutes) and just write. Don't stop to fix sentences, look things up, or organize. The goal is to get your thinking onto the page. You might start with something like "I think the most interesting convention I noticed is..." and just keep going. You can clean it up later — right now you're just getting the ideas out of your head and into a document.

If you get stuck, try freewriting about one specific convention or one section of the paper at a time instead of trying to write the whole thing from start to finish.

Working Outline Method

Start by creating an outline of the major sections of your paper (use the Example Organization page if you need a starting point). Then go into each section and write down what ideas and evidence you plan to use — which samples you'll reference, what points you want to make, what quotes or details you'll pull in.

Once you've filled in the outline with your ideas and evidence, start writing out the paragraphs within each section. You don't have to go in order — start with whatever section feels easiest. The outline keeps growing and filling in until it becomes a draft.

Either method works. The point is to start getting words on the page without worrying about whether they're good yet. That's what Lamott is talking about — you can't revise a blank page, but you can always revise a messy one.
