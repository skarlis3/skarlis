How to Analyze a Genre

ENGL 1181 — Reading

In Week 3, you chose a genre from your field and started noticing some things about it — its purpose, audience, and context. This week, you're going deeper. You're moving from noticing to analyzing: looking closely at how the genre works and identifying the conventions that define it.

Genre analysis means examining a type of writing to understand the patterns and expectations that shape it. When we say a genre has "conventions," we mean the recurring features that readers and writers in that community have come to expect — things like how the document is organized, what kind of tone it uses, what information it includes (and leaves out), and how it looks on the page.

Why Multiple Samples Matter

You can't figure out a genre's conventions from a single example. One sample only tells you what one person did one time. To understand what the genre itself tends to do, you need to look at several samples and compare them.

When you look across multiple samples, you're looking for trends and patterns — the things that show up again and again, not just in one document but across several. For example, if you're analyzing demand letters and you notice that every sample you find opens by identifying the legal issue and closes with a specific action the recipient needs to take, that's a convention of the genre. If only one of your samples does something unusual — say, includes a personal anecdote — that's probably a choice that individual author made, not a feature of the genre itself.

This is the difference between analyzing a genre and analyzing a single text. A single text can surprise you, break rules, or do something creative. But the genre is the pattern underneath — the set of expectations that the text is either following or pushing against. You need multiple samples to see that pattern.

What to Look For

As you compare your samples, pay attention to both the writing and the design. Here are the main areas to focus on:

  • Content: What information gets included? Is there anything that every sample includes? Anything that's always left out? How detailed is the information?
  • Organization: How is the information arranged? Do the samples follow a similar structure or order? For instance, do they all start with the same kind of opening, or present information in the same sequence?
  • Tone and Style: How does the writing sound? Is it formal, casual, technical, reassuring? Is it written in first person, third person? Are sentences long and complex or short and direct?
  • Design and Layout: How does the document look? What fonts, spacing, or formatting choices are common? Are there headers, bullet points, tables, images? How does the visual design support what the document is trying to do?

Not every genre will have strong conventions in every category. Some genres are defined more by their structure (like a legal brief), while others are defined more by their tone or visual design (like a marketing email). Part of your analysis is figuring out which conventions matter most for your genre.
