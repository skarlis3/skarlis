Part 1: Collect Your Samples

Find at least three new samples of the genre you chose in Week 3 (not the same one you used for Week 3 — find three different ones). These can be real examples, templates, or samples from professional organizations or training resources — anything that shows you what this genre looks like in practice. Try to find samples from different sources so you get a range.

Upload all three samples to Canvas along with your written observations. Screenshots or PDFs are both fine — see the How to Save a Sample as a PDF instructions from Week 3 if you need a refresher.

Part 2: Identify Conventions

Look at your samples side by side. Your goal is to start figuring out the conventions of this genre — the things that are consistent across your samples, not just features of one individual document.

Use the How to Analyze a Genre reading and the Genre Analysis Handout to guide you. Focus on what your samples have in common:

  • Content: What information shows up in all (or most) of your samples? Is there anything that every sample includes? Anything that's always left out?
  • Organization: Do your samples follow a similar structure? Is information presented in the same order?
  • Tone and Style: How does the writing sound across your samples? Is the tone consistent? What about the level of formality, the point of view, or the sentence style?
  • Design and Layout: How do your samples look? Are there similarities in formatting, fonts, spacing, use of headers, images, or other visual elements?

Your observations can be in list form or short paragraphs — whatever helps you organize your thinking. The key is to focus on the similarities between your samples, because that's where the conventions are.

Submit as a Word document or text entry on Canvas. Upload your three (or more) genre samples as separate files.
