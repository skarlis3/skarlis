TED Paragraphs

Building Strong Body Paragraphs

What Is TED?

Every body paragraph in your essay should do three things:

  1. T — Topic Sentence: Start with a clear sentence that tells the reader what this paragraph is about. Think of it as a mini-thesis for the paragraph. It should connect back to your overall argument.
  2. E — Evidence: Bring in your sources. This is where you use paraphrases, summaries, or short quotes from your genre samples to support the point you just made.
  3. D — Discussion: Explain what the evidence means. Don't just drop in a source and move on — tell the reader why this evidence matters, how it connects to your topic sentence, and what it shows about the bigger picture.

In practice, evidence and discussion often alternate. You might introduce one piece of evidence, discuss it briefly, then bring in another and discuss how it compares. That's normal and good — the key is that you're not just listing evidence back-to-back without explaining what it means.

Example Paragraph

Below is an example body paragraph analyzing conventions of PowerPoint presentations used for teaching. This is a different genre than the one you're writing about — it's here to show you what the TED structure looks like in action for a genre analysis essay.

Example body paragraph

TOne of the most consistent conventions across teaching PowerPoints is the use of minimal text paired with large headers to guide students through the material without overwhelming them. EIn all three of the lecture slide decks I collected, slides rarely had more than four or five bullet points, and most of the bullet points were short phrases rather than full sentences. For example, in Dr. Patel's Introduction to Biology slides, the header "Cell Division: Key Stages" was followed by just three bullet points: "Interphase," "Mitosis," and "Cytokinesis" — each with a two-to-four word description beside it (Patel). Professor Hernandez's Business Communication slides followed the same pattern, using headers like "Barriers to Listening" with short phrases underneath instead of paragraphs of explanation (Hernandez). Even in the third sample, a set of slides from a professional development workshop for teachers, the slides used large bold headers and kept the text to what could be read in about five seconds (Oakley). DThis pattern makes sense when you consider the purpose of lecture slides: they're not meant to be the lecture itself, but a visual guide that runs alongside whatever the instructor is saying out loud. If the slides had too much text, students would be reading instead of listening. By keeping the text minimal, the slides let the instructor control the pace and fill in the details verbally, while students use the slides as a framework to organize their notes around.

This example was created by Claude AI for instructional purposes only. All names and sources are fictional. It's here to show what a TED paragraph looks like — not to serve as a model of actual research.

The Breakdown

Here's the same paragraph broken into its TED parts:

T — Topic Sentence

"One of the most consistent conventions across teaching PowerPoints is the use of minimal text paired with large headers to guide students through the material without overwhelming them."

This sentence tells the reader exactly what the paragraph will analyze: a specific, observable convention (minimal text + large headers). It's not just "structure" — it's a concrete pattern.

E — Evidence (Sample 1)

"In Dr. Patel's Introduction to Biology slides, the header 'Cell Division: Key Stages' was followed by just three bullet points: 'Interphase,' 'Mitosis,' and 'Cytokinesis' — each with a two-to-four word description beside it (Patel)."

A specific example from one sample, with enough detail that the reader can see the convention in action.

E — Evidence (Sample 2)

"Professor Hernandez's Business Communication slides followed the same pattern, using headers like 'Barriers to Listening' with short phrases underneath instead of paragraphs of explanation (Hernandez)."

A second sample showing the same pattern. This is how you demonstrate that something is a convention — it shows up across multiple samples, not just one.

E — Evidence (Sample 3)

"Even in the third sample, a set of slides from a professional development workshop for teachers, the slides used large bold headers and kept the text to what could be read in about five seconds (Oakley)."

A third sample from a different context (professional development vs. college classes), which strengthens the argument that this is a convention of the genre, not just one person's preference.

D — Discussion

"This pattern makes sense when you consider the purpose of lecture slides: they're not meant to be the lecture itself, but a visual guide that runs alongside whatever the instructor is saying out loud..."

The discussion explains why this convention exists — it connects the pattern back to the purpose of the genre. It doesn't just repeat what the samples showed; it tells the reader what it means.

Tips for Using TED

  • Evidence and Discussion can alternate. You don't have to dump all your evidence first and then discuss. It's often more natural to bring in a source, comment on it, then bring in the next one.
  • Discussion is not a summary. Don't just restate what your samples showed. Tell the reader why it matters and what it means for your argument.
  • Your topic sentence should be your own idea. Don't start a paragraph with evidence. Start with the point you want to make, then use your samples to support it.
  • Use specific details from your samples. Don't just say "all three samples had short bullet points." Show the reader what you mean with concrete examples.
  • Multiple samples strengthen your point. If a pattern shows up in all of your samples, that's strong evidence it's a convention. Show that by pulling examples from more than one sample per paragraph.
