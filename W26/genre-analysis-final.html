About Revision

Revision is all about taking a fresh set of eyes to previously created materials. Although this may sound like glorified proofreading, revision is a whole heap more than that, and to be done effectively, revision requires time, organization, and attention to detail. The danger with revision lies in the temptation to believe you already know all the ins and outs of your work, believing there is nothing left to "do" to it.

Editing is the final step as part of the revision, but revision is not editing. Revision is revisiting, rereading, rethinking, reworking, re-seeing.

Steps for Revision

  1. Start with your feedback. Read through the feedback from our conferences on your drafts. This is your starting point — not your entire revision plan, but the foundation for one.
  2. Get additional samples if needed. If you need to gather any additional IRAC brief samples, do it at the start of the revision (not later).
  3. Create a revision plan. Identify major areas (high/middle-order concerns) first, then minor areas (later-order concerns). What needs the most work? What's a quick fix?
  4. Use your resources. Visit the Academic Success Center for tutoring help, or review the feedback you've received throughout the semester.
  5. Revise your draft. Expect a non-linear process — you'll find yourself going back and forth between your samples and your paper, reworking sections, and revisiting earlier decisions. Plan for substantial work.
  6. Proofread. Once you are done with your revision, proofread your paper. You can use tools like the free version of Grammarly or GenAI tools to help with proofreading. If you use GenAI for proofreading, be cautious with the output to make sure it doesn't strip out your writing voice or change your ideas.

What I'm Looking For

  • Meaningful revision since the rough draft — not just proofreading, but substantive changes that strengthen the paper
  • Well organized with a clear structure
  • Claims are well supported by evidence from your IRAC brief samples
  • Evidence is integrated — samples are woven into your analysis, not just dropped in
  • Readable with clear sentence flow and paragraph structure
  • Generally proofread and cleaned up
  • MLA formatting — in-text citations and Works Cited follow MLA conventions

Grading

The Genre Analysis Final Draft is worth 20% of your course grade.

Grade: A (95%)

The essay meets all assignment requirements, demonstrates meaningful revision since the rough draft, is well organized, and claims are well supported by evidence from your IRAC brief samples. The essay is readable, generally proofread, and follows MLA formatting and citation conventions.

Grade: B (85%)

The essay meets assignment requirements, but one area isn't quite working — for example, uneven organization, thin evidence integration, or revision that doesn't go deep enough in some sections.

Grade: C (75%)

The essay meets the basic assignment requirements but has multiple areas that aren't fully working. The work is present but doesn't fully deliver on the criteria described above.

Grade: D (65%)

An attempt at the essay is clear, but there are major issues. Requirements may be partially met, but significant gaps in revision, organization, evidence use, or other criteria are present.

Grade: E

The essay does not meet the assignment requirements or has critical gaps.

An E grade will fall between 0% and 55% depending on the extent of the missing or unmet requirements.

No Credit (0%)

The final draft was not submitted.

Note: An A receives a 95%. Scores above 95% are reserved for work that demonstrates exceptional effort above and beyond the criteria described above.
