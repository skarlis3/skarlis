Genre Analysis

ENGL 1181 — Handout

Genre conventions are the patterns and tendencies that define how a type of writing works — the features that show up consistently across multiple examples. Use these questions to identify the conventions of your genre. For a completed example, see the interactive cover letter example below.

For rhetorical situation questions (purpose, audience, author, content, context), see the Rhetorical Analysis Handout.

Conventions of the Genre

Click a category to see the analysis questions.

  • What do you notice about the writing in this genre? For example, is the tone:

    • conversational?
    • authoritative? detached?
    • informational? persuasive/argumentative?
    • logical? measured (i.e. thoughtful and unbiased)? inflammatory? (think: click-bait news article titles)
    • humorous? serious? contemplative?

    Does the author use "I," "we," "you," or keep things in third person? Does the text speak directly to the reader?

    Is the style more direct and assertive (i.e. researchers found, this proves, etc.)? Does the author use hedging or clarifying words (i.e. this suggests, this may mean, etc...)

  • How is the information organized? What is the order the information is presented? (For example: Does it start with the first thing that happened and then go to the next? Is it summarized first and then explained?)

    Tip: Try a quick reverse outline to map the organization of a piece.

    1. Go through paragraph by paragraph and write one short phrase describing what each paragraph does — not what it says, but its job (e.g., "introduces the problem," "gives a personal example," "provides data").
    2. Then ask yourself: Do your genre samples all seem to follow the same order? Why might the ideas or information be presented in this order? Are there sections that every example of this genre seems to include? Which seem to be optional (or context-specific)?

  • How is the genre formatted? What kind of fonts are used? Are there images? If there are, how do they relate to the text and message? Are colors used? If so, how? Are there any other design elements? What impact does the design and layout have on the overall message?

  • For genres that are persuasive: how does the author use ethos, pathos and logos to appeal to the audience? Most persuasive texts blend all three — look for which one seems to dominate and why that might suit the genre.

    Ethos: an appeal to the credibility, experience, or authority of the author

    • Citing professional credentials or years of experience ("As a pediatrician with 20 years of practice...")
    • Referencing respected sources or institutions
    • Acknowledging counterarguments or limitations, which builds trust through honesty

    Pathos: an appeal to emotion

    • Telling a personal story or anecdote to make the reader feel connected to the issue
    • Using vivid, sensory language that puts the reader "in the scene"
    • Choosing words with strong emotional connotations ("devastating" vs. "unfortunate," "thriving" vs. "doing well")
    • Including images, testimonials, or case studies that humanize a problem

    Logos: an appeal to logic

    • Presenting statistics, data, or research findings as evidence
    • Showing how one event or idea leads logically to the next
    • Using comparisons or analogies to make a complex argument easier to follow
    • Structuring the argument in a clear, step-by-step progression where each point builds on the last

    Feeling stuck? Sometimes, it can be just as helpful to look at what a genre isn't doing and/or to compare it to another genre. For example, what do text messages do that this genre doesn't? Or how is this genre different than an academic essay? Then use that as a starting place to figure out what the genre is doing.

December 10, 2018

Ms. Susan Carey
Senior Manager
Wholesale Wine USA
23 Green St.
Boston, MA 02116

Dear Ms. Carey:

I'm writing to express my interest in the sales associate position at Wholesale Wine USA (listed on Crimson Careers), which I believe reports to you.

Having worked in the industry for nearly eight years, I now have a deep love and appreciation for wine and cuisine. At Shay's Pub and Wine Bar, nothing made me happier than speaking with customers about the perfect wine to pair with their orders. The result was a 25% increase in sales and 30% increase in repeat customers.

My experience at Aspen's award-winning Montagna allowed me to expand upon my knowledge of wine, locally inspired cuisine and the highest standards of service. Our weekly blind-tastings fueled my desire to further myself in this field, and I'm currently in the process of acquiring certification through both the Court of Master Sommeliers and the Wine Spirit & Education Trust.

I also just returned from France, where I worked on an organic vineyard in Beaujolais and was able to shadow the vigneron who ran the estate, the largest of its kind in the region. As Wholesale Wine USA begins to expand into international markets, I'm confident that my knowledge of the French wine industry can help contribute to your company's growth and success.

I've attached my resume and am readily available via phone or email. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any questions. Thank you for your consideration and I look forward to hearing back from you!

Sincerely,
Georgina Santiago
