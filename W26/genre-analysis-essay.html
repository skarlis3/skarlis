Genre Analysis Essay

Objective & Overview

For this assignment, you will write a 1,300–1,700 word (roughly 5–7 page) essay that analyzes the IRAC brief as a genre used in legal practice. Your essay should analyze this genre—including its conventions—and explain how it functions within the professional context of legal work: how it helps legal professionals achieve their goals, and what it reveals about the field's values, expectations, and communication practices.

You have already done much of the groundwork for this paper through your earlier assignments. Your field exploration, genre selection, and sample collection are the raw material for this essay—now you're building an analysis around what you've observed.

Assignment Prompt

Your analysis should include:

  • A brief introduction to the professional context (the paralegal/legal field and where IRAC briefs fit in legal practice)
  • A description of the genre
  • The purpose and audience (generalized from your samples and observations)
  • An analysis of at least three conventions of the genre
  • An analysis of how this genre functions in professional practice

Help with the Analysis

The sections below are here to help you think through each part of the prompt. These are not prompts to answer in order—they're questions to help you develop your analysis. Expand whichever ones are useful to you.

Introducing the Professional Context

Provide a brief overview of the legal field—specifically, the kind of work where IRAC briefs are used—before introducing the genre itself. Your reader needs enough context to understand why this genre exists and who uses it. You don't need to write a history of the legal profession; just set the stage.

Describing the Genre

Provide an overview of what an IRAC brief is and any background information readers would need to understand it. If there are variations in how IRAC briefs are structured or used in different contexts (e.g., law school assignments vs. professional practice, different areas of law), describe the range and then clarify which version you're focusing on.

Analyzing Purpose & Audience

Purpose: What is the purpose of this genre? What problem does it solve or what need does it address in legal work? How does it help legal professionals organize their thinking, communicate with others, or achieve specific goals?

Audience: Who reads IRAC briefs? Are there expectations about who writes them and who receives them? Does the audience change depending on the context (e.g., a brief written for a supervising attorney vs. one written for a court)?

Analyzing Conventions

Conventions are the informal rules and patterns that define how a genre works. If "conventions" feels abstract, mentally substitute "informal rules." Ask yourself: if I had to write an IRAC brief from scratch, what would I need to do to make it look and sound like a real one? The patterns you've noticed across your samples—those are the conventions.

You need to analyze at least three conventions. A convention is something specific and observable—not a broad category like "tone" or "structure," but a concrete pattern you can point to in your samples. For example, a convention of patient chart notes might be: "Patient chart notes use standardized abbreviations like 'pt' for patient, 'hx' for history, and 'dx' for diagnosis instead of writing out full words, which allows for faster notes."

To find conventions, look at your samples and ask: what specific things keep showing up? Here are some areas to look in:

  • Structure/Organization: How is the document organized? What sections appear and in what order?
  • Tone & Language: What kind of language is used? Are there specific phrases, terms, or ways of saying things that show up consistently?
  • Style & Approach: How are arguments built? How is evidence presented?
  • Design & Format: Are there formatting patterns (headings, citations, length, visual layout)?

These are places to look, not the conventions themselves. The conventions are the specific patterns you find within these areas.

For each convention, support your analysis with evidence from your samples and discuss why that convention likely exists (what purpose does it serve?).

Tip: Don't try to cover all three conventions in one paragraph. Break them into multiple, fully developed paragraphs with support from your samples.

How the Genre Functions in Professional Practice

This is where you step back and discuss what the genre means in the bigger picture of legal work. Some possible angles:

  • Professional Values: What does this genre reveal about what the legal field values? (Precision? Logical reasoning? Thoroughness? Efficiency?) How does the genre's structure reflect those values?
  • Professional Communication: How does this genre connect to other forms of communication in legal practice? What role does it play in a larger workflow?
  • What Happens When Conventions Aren't Followed: What would happen if someone wrote an IRAC brief that didn't follow these conventions? What does that tell you about why the conventions matter?
Supporting Your Claims & Using Evidence

Support your claims with evidence from your samples. For convention paragraphs, include more than one example to show a pattern (not just one instance).

  • Quotes work best for language and tone conventions (e.g., specific phrasing or legal terminology).
  • Detailed descriptions work best for structural conventions (e.g., the order of sections, how arguments are built).
  • Comparisons across samples are especially effective—showing that the same pattern appears in multiple briefs strengthens your analysis.
