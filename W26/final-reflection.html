Final Reflection

ENGL 1181 — Due: Sunday, May 10

The Final Reflection is a reflection on your growth, strengths, and learning this semester. You'll curate portfolio artifacts from your work — excerpts, screenshots, before/after comparisons — and reflect on what they show about you as a writer and student. This is separate from your final genre analysis essay, which is submitted on its own.

This is a multimodal project. Your reflection should demonstrate an understanding of visual rhetoric — the design choices you make (layout, color, spacing, images, how you present artifacts) should be intentional and purposeful, not just default slides with text. Before you start building, complete the readings and videos listed under Readings below so you have a foundation for those choices.

Format Options

Choose one:

  1. PowerPoint or Google Slides — Build your reflection as a slide deck.
  2. Google Site (or another website) — Build your reflection as a webpage. If you use something other than Google Sites, just make sure I can access it via a link and that you can embed documents or screenshots.

Want to use a different format? Check with me first.

What Are Portfolio Artifacts?

Portfolio artifacts are pieces of evidence from your own work in this class — anything that demonstrates something about your writing, learning, or growth. This could be a before/after snippet from your genre analysis essay, notes from our meetings, a screenshot of your planning or revision process, your sample collection observations, or anything else from your work this semester.

What Makes a Thoughtful and Well-Developed Reflection?

A strong reflection goes beyond summarizing what you did. It shows that you're thinking critically about your own work and growth. Here's the difference:

  • Surface-level: "I revised my paper and made it better."
  • Thoughtful: "I noticed my argument was stronger when I reorganized my conventions by how important they were to the genre instead of just listing them in the order I noticed them. It helped me build a more logical case for why the genre works the way it does."

A well-developed reflection also means:

  • Your artifacts are well chosen and actually demonstrate the point you're making — don't just grab something random; pick something that clearly shows what you're reflecting on
  • All six sections are present and substantive
  • You're specific — use examples from your own writing to illustrate your points rather than speaking in generalities

Reflection Prompts

1. Growth as a Writer (Before & After)

Artifact required: Yes

Show something from earlier in the semester that represents a starting point in your writing — this could be from your literacy narrative, a reflection, or your early genre analysis drafts. Then show something from later in the semester that demonstrates growth in comparison.

Reflect on: What changed? What do you notice about the difference between the two? What do you think contributed to that growth?

2. A Strength (Artifact + Reflection)

Artifact required: Yes

Show an artifact that demonstrates something you think you do well as a writer. This could be a high-order concern like organization, building a logical argument, or using evidence effectively — a middle-order concern like flow, transitions, or voice — or even something about genre awareness or rhetorical choices.

Reflect on: What is the strength you're highlighting? How do you know this is something you do well? When did you start to notice it or develop confidence in it?

3. Revision (Before & After)

Artifact required: Yes

Show a specific passage, section, or element of your writing before and after revision. This could come from your partial draft vs. rough draft, your rough draft vs. final draft, or any point in the semester where you revised something and it got better.

Reflect on: What did you change and why? What made this revision work? What did you learn about revision from doing this?

4. Growth as a Student (Flexible)

Artifact required: Yes

This one is more open. Show an artifact that demonstrates how you grew as a student this semester — not just as a writer, but in how you approach learning, writing, or college in general.

Some examples of what this could look like:

  • A screenshot of your planning or revision process
  • Evidence of how you managed the independent study structure — setting your own pace, staying on track, communicating about your work
  • Something that shows how your approach to feedback changed over the semester
  • Evidence of learning to use a tool or resource (MLA formatting, revision strategies, analyzing genres, etc.)
  • Something that shows a shift in how you approach writing assignments or your own learning

These are just examples — if you have a different way to show your growth as a student, go for it.

Reflect on: What changed about how you approach school, learning, or this class specifically? Why does this artifact represent that change?

5. Advice for Future Students

Artifact required: No

What advice would you give to a student taking ENGL 1181 next semester? What do you wish you'd known at the start?

6. Final Thoughts

Artifact required: No

Choose one (or more) of the following prompts:

  • What is the one thing you want to keep working on with your writing?
  • What do you think the most helpful thing you learned this semester is?
  • What would you do differently if you were to do it over? Why? What would you do the same?
  • Any final thoughts on the class and your work in general?
