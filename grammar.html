Grammar & Style Toolkit

Grammar isn't just about following rules; it's about clarity. In First-Year Writing, we focus less on "policing" language and more on ensuring your ideas aren't obstructed by mechanical roadblocks.

1. The "Big Three" Common Errors

If you master these three concepts, you will solve 90% of the sentence-level issues in your college papers.

1. The Fragment
An incomplete sentence that lacks a subject, a verb, or a complete thought.

The Error: "Because I drank four Red Bulls."
The Fix: "I can see sounds because I drank four Red Bulls." (Connected to a main clause).
2. The Run-on
Two independent sentences smushed together without punctuation.

The Error: "I wrote this at 3 AM the printer is broken."
The Fix: "I wrote this at 3 AM. The printer is broken." (Separated by a period).
3. The Comma Splice
Two independent sentences joined only by a comma (the most common college error!).

The Error: "The market only has stale pretzels, I am going to starve."
The Fix: "The market only has stale pretzels, so I am going to starve." (Added a conjunction).

2. Trusted External Resources

Don't rely on random Google searches. These are the gold-standard resources for college-level writing.

  • Khan Academy Grammar
    A comprehensive, free course that breaks down everything from parts of speech to complex syntax. Great for visual learners.
  • UNC Chapel Hill Writing Center
    Their "Tips & Tools" section is excellent. They offer short, readable handouts on specific issues like "Passive Voice" or "Flow."

3. GenAI & Your Writing Voice

Generative AI (like ChatGPT) can be a useful tool, but it can also flatten your writing style. You need to constrain the AI so it doesn't delete your personality.

1. Draft First, AI Second
Never start with a blank page and an AI prompt. Write your messy first draft yourself. Only use AI to critique what you have already written.
2. Constrain the AI (The "Hands Off" Prompt)
If you ask AI to "fix this," it will rewrite your entire paragraph to sound like a robot. Instead, give it strict boundaries:

"Please proofread the following paragraph for grammar errors only. List the errors you find, but DO NOT rewrite the paragraph or change my tone. I want to make the changes myself."
3. The "Read Aloud" Test
After you make edits, read your paper out loud. If you stumble over a word or if a sentence sounds too fancy for your normal speaking voice, the AI probably inserted it. Cut it out. Your professor wants to hear your voice, not a machine's.
